- March 11, 2003, Paper maker Boise Cascade warns its first-quarter loss will be worse than expected and says it will cut 700 jobs as it struggles with $10 million increase in energy costs; will limit spending and freeze salaries.
- April 5, 2003, UPM-Kymmene, the world’s third-biggest paper manufacturer, said first-quarter profit fell by about 50 percent from the period a year earlier, hurt by lower paper prices and a weaker dollar. Demand for UPM’s products is weak, the chief executive, Juha Niemelae, said. A 20 percent drop in the value of the dollar against the euro in the last year has crimped North American sales, which generate 14 percent of revenue. The company’s credit outlook was revised to negative by Standard & Poor’s.
- May 15, 2003, The MeadWestvaco Corporation, the maker of coated paper used in magazines, said yesterday that it would take a $13 million charge this quarter for management changes that included replacing its finance chief. The company disclosed the expense in a regulatory filing and was not specific about what costs it had incurred. MeadWestvaco will also record an expense of $2 million to close three plants. By the end of June the company will shut plants in Virginia, Tennessee and Delaware. The company has cut 4,000 jobs since it was formed in the January 2002 merger of the Mead and Westvaco corporations.
- September 11, 2003, The International Paper Company said yesterday that it would cut about 3,000 jobs, or 3.5 percent of its global work force, over the next year as part of its previously announced $1.5 billion effort to improve profit. A spokeswoman, Jennifer Boardman, said the company would make the reductions across its salaried work force, with about 450 positions being cut at its operational headquarters in Memphis. Ms. Boardman said some cuts would be made by not filling open positions and through attrition. The company, which is based in Stamford, Conn., and is the world’s largest maker of forest products, currently, has about 86,500 employees.
